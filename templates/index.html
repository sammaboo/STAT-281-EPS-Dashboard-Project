Estimate Convergence
Story Point
Each dot = one monthly consensus estimate for one quarter. Green dots = beat quarters, red = miss. The blue trend line shows the average forecast error at each lead time. The x-axis shows months before earnings. Stars at x=0 mark the actual. Tight convergence = predictable company.
Analyst Dispersion
Story Point
Measures analyst disagreement — the standard deviation of individual EPS estimates divided by the absolute consensus value. Higher dispersion means less agreement among analysts. Spikes in dispersion often precede earnings surprises or periods of uncertainty.

EPS Predictability
Story Point
Scatter plot of lagged EPS (t-1) vs. current EPS (t) with an OLS regression line. A tight cluster along the line means earnings are highly persistent and predictable. Points far from the line indicate volatile or one-off earnings events. R-squared measures how much of current EPS is explained by last quarter's EPS.

EPS & Stock Returns Analysis
Surprise vs. Returns
Story Point
Tests the PEAD hypothesis cross-sectionally: do larger earnings surprises produce larger stock returns? Each dot is one earnings event. The OLS trendline shows the market-wide relationship between surprise % and next-quarter return.
EPS & Returns Trend
Story Point
Dual-axis time series — EPS on the left axis (bars), cumulative stock return on the right (line). Shows whether a company's earnings growth translates into stock price appreciation over time.
Post-Earnings Announcement Drift
Post-Earnings Announcement Drift
Story Point
Tests whether stock prices continue drifting in the direction of the earnings surprise after the announcement. Positive surprises (beats) should see upward drift; negative surprises (misses) should see downward drift. Persistent drift suggests the market underreacts to earnings news, creating a tradeable anomaly.
